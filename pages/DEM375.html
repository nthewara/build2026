So you want to become an MVP?

🏢 Microsoft Build 2026 🎤 Betsy Weber, Fernanda Saraiva, + MVP panel (Jeremy Sinclair, Christine, and others) ⏱️ 26m

🎯 TL;DR

A community/career talk demystifying the Microsoft MVP (Most Valuable Professional) award, run by two Microsoft program insiders — Betsy Weber and Fernanda Saraiva (herself a former MVP). MVP is not a job, a Microsoft label, or a media-influencer deal — it's Microsoft's annual thank-you and recognition for independent community leaders who, on their own time, share their technical expertise and real-world product knowledge and feed honest feedback back to Microsoft's product groups. The program is 30+ years old, 4,000+ MVPs across 96 countries and ~49–50 languages, spanning 100+ technologies. The core message boils down to three simple steps: be an expert, do what you love (and share it), and let us know (get nominated) — and the throughline advice from real MVPs is to contribute out of genuine passion, because the people who get re-awarded year after year are the ones who'd do it regardless of the badge.

🔑 Key Takeaways

  • MVP = Most Valuable Professional — an award, not a job title, given by Microsoft to recognise exceptional community leaders who share technical expertise and real-world Microsoft product/service knowledge.
  • It's recognition + a thank-you, not employment: MVPs do their community work on their own time, not as part of a day job they're paid for.
  • The program is over 30 years old and a genuinely global landscape: 4,000+ MVPs ("influencers"), 96 countries, ~49–50 languages.
  • It now covers more than 100 technologies, grouped by affinity into award categories (e.g. BizApps → Dynamics 365 / Copilot Studio / Power Platform; Windows/developer; etc.).
  • No single contribution is mandatory — you do NOT have to blog, podcast, or contribute to open source. You contribute in the ways you want to; passion dictates the format.
  • Microsoft's program philosophy is "meet you where you are" — start with what you know and enjoy, for the greater good of helping your community grow.
  • MVPs are distinct from media/paid influencers — the motivation is passion for community and helping others, not paid promotion.
  • You must be nominated — either by a Microsoft employee or by an existing MVP. Self-nomination is also a valid starting point.
  • It's a year-long award, re-evaluated annually: re-awarded MVPs are the ones who do what they love and would keep doing it with or without the badge.
  • Eligibility: you generally can't be a full-time Microsoft employee to be an MVP (it's independent of Microsoft), though vendors can qualify; many MVPs are later hired by Microsoft (both presenters' co-hosts and Fernanda herself followed that path).
  • Benefits: recognition/credibility ("badge of honor"), NDA/insider info & roadmaps before the public, direct product-group connections, a two-way feedback channel to Microsoft, a global peer network, exclusive event invites (incl. the annual MVP Summit in Redmond), and personal/professional growth.
  • 100+ MVPs participate at Build — speaking, helping run labs, and other activities.
  • Three simple steps to become an MVP: (1) Be an expert, (2) Do what you love — and share it, (3) Let us know (get nominated).
  • Contribution formats are broad: blogging, podcasting, live streaming/live coding on Twitch, videos, open-source work, content creation, community boards — and you can be an open-source or content-creation expert, not just a "Microsoft person."
  • Real-MVP advice on getting started: do stuff you're passionate about and share it — with a person, your local community, or community boards — and work–life–community balance is a real, ongoing struggle (you fit it in because you love it).
  • Resource to learn more & how to contribute: aka.ms/mvp/learn, plus the MVP website to browse the 4,000+ MVPs and the full list of products/award categories.

📚 Detailed Notes

Who's presenting (and why it's credible)

The session is co-hosted by two people inside the program: - Betsy Weber — Senior Engagement Lead at Microsoft, working with the MVP program; 10 years at Microsoft as of the month of the talk. - Fernanda Saraiva — Principal Customer Experience Program Manager and a former MVP herself (was an MVP before being hired ~14 years ago), with ~15 years helping the MVP program grow. She explicitly speaks from both sides — community member and program insider.

This dual perspective frames the whole talk: the "official" definition from the program, sanity-checked against lived MVP experience. Later they bring up a panel of real MVPs (Jeremy Sinclair; Christine) to add first-hand journeys — though microphone failures dogged the panel, so several MVP answers in the transcript are partial. (Panel speaker names beyond Jeremy and Christine are caption-uncertain.)

What "MVP" actually means

MVP stands for Most Valuable Professional. The Microsoft MVP program is an award Microsoft gives to recognise exceptional community leaders who: - Share their passion with the community, - Share their technical expertise and real-world knowledge of Microsoft products and services with others, and - Share their feedback with Microsoft.

So an MVP is someone who has demonstrated knowledge and understanding of Microsoft products/services and has actively contributed to the community. They're characterised as very passionate about Microsoft products and technology. Crucially, MVP is framed as a recognition / thank-you, not a credential you buy or a role you're hired into.

The landscape — scale & global reach

Key program facts the presenters wanted the room to anchor on: - 30+ years old — a long-established program. - More than 4,000 MVPs (referred to affectionately as "influencers," in the community-leader sense). - 96 countries of coverage. - A little over 49, almost 50 languages. - The program actively looks for communities that are growing and blooming in every part of the world and invites locals to step up.

Fernanda's invitation to the audience: if you know MVPs, know your community, are building one, or have joined one — you could become "the 4,001st" person in that number.

The program's philosophy: "meet you where you are"

The stated foundation of the program is to meet you where you are. Once you start contributing, you should do what makes you happy, what you're passionate about, and what you have good knowledge about. The underlying motivations they describe (and want to see) are intrinsic: - "I'm doing something for the greater good." - "I'm sharing with my friends / helping a community grow together." - "I want to learn and get to know more about products." - "I want to support my friends and my community."

The professional/personal recognition that comes back to you is framed as a byproduct, not the goal.

What it takes — the criteria

To be considered, the program looks for impactful, significant activities delivered across a specific period of time. The main ingredients: - Technical expertise — you genuinely know what you're talking about and want to go deeper into a specific topic. - Leadership — but not in the "top-100 influencer" sense. It means doing what you're passionate about and building up your ecosystem — a group of friends / like-minded folks who want to grow together. You can start with your own company or even the people in the room/session. - Helping build the products — once you're an MVP you're exposed to product-group engagements and lots of people across Microsoft, where you tell your experience, share what you learned, troubleshoot, give feedback, and contribute to the future of solutions — keeping the cycle feeding and growing over time.

If you don't know where to start, the advice is to look at the MVP website and find one of the 4,000+ leaders to learn from.

The myth-busting Q&A (what is NOT required)

Betsy plays the skeptic and Fernanda answers — the point is that specific activities are optional, not mandatory: - "Do I have to have a blog?" → A good start, but not mandatory. - "Do I have to have a podcast?" → No — only if it's your passion. - "Do I have to contribute to open source?" → Only if it's your passion; you'll have opportunities, but it's not required.

The takeaway Betsy lands: contribute in the ways you want to. "If you want to blog, blog. If you want to podcast, podcast. If you want to live stream on Twitch and show how you're live coding, do that." Give back your way.

MVPs vs. media influencers (an important distinction)

The presenters are explicit that MVPs are community leaders, different from media/paid influencers: - MVPs do this on their own time. - It's not part of their day job or what they're paid for in their profession. - They do it because they're passionate about community and want to help others. - The award is simply Microsoft's way of giving recognition and a thank-you to those community leaders.

Eligibility — can a Microsoft employee be an MVP?

  • MVP is voluntary and totally independent of Microsoft — independent of Microsoft labels and of employee eligibility.
  • You cannot be a full-time Microsoft employee and be an MVP.
  • You can be a vendor at Microsoft and still qualify (the carve-out is specifically about not being a full-time employee).
  • A common arc: people contribute and learn in the community as MVPs, and later get hired by Microsoft — Fernanda is a living example (former MVP, hired ~14 years ago). This is why she can speak to "both passions and both sides."

Award categories & technology coverage

  • The program covers more than 100 technologies, grouped by affinity into award categories.
  • Example from the panel: "BizApps" maps to Dynamics 365, Copilot Studio, and Power Platform; "Windows" sits on the development side (and a Windows MVP may also hold a developer technology category).
  • MVPs' passions can overlap multiple areas — part of what the presenters say is great about the community is that "you can be whatever you want to be and do the technology you're associated with."
  • To see the full picture, they repeatedly point to the MVP website for the complete list of products and how they're organised into award categories.

Benefits — why become an MVP?

Betsy's rundown of what you achieve: - Recognition & credibility — the award is a "badge of honor" that can open doors. - Build presence — 100+ MVPs participate at Build (speaking, running/helping with labs, and other activities). - NDA / insider information before the public — you get connected to product groups, shown roadmaps, and given insider information. - A feedback channel — because you're in Microsoft products all day with real clients, you can share what users are running into straight to the product groups. - A global community — join 4,000+ MVPs; wherever you travel, there's likely an MVP to meet up with. - Exclusive invites to events and conferences, including the annual MVP Summit — MVPs come to campus in Redmond (or join online) to meet product groups in person, hear what's coming next, and share input. - Personal & professional growth.

The panel MVPs reinforced the community/relationships benefit most strongly: the recognition that there are so many people around the world, the close personal friends made, and the practical superpower that you can "pick any country and have friends there" — or post a specific problem to the community and someone will help.

Real-MVP advice — getting started, balance, and staying awarded

From the (mic-challenged) panel and the hosts: - How to get started in the community: "Do stuff that you're passionate about — and share it." Share with somebody else, your local community, and get on the community boards. - Nomination path in practice: You can get nominated into the community; an existing MVP who knows you can nominate you — and you can also self-nominate first. But "those other things" (the actual contributing) still have to be done. - The re-award insight (Betsy): It's a year-long award. The MVPs who get re-awarded every year are the ones who do what they love because they love it — they're not chasing the award and would keep going whether they got it or not. (Strongly implied advice: chase the contribution, not the badge.) - Work–life–community balance: Acknowledged as a real struggle, especially at the pace community work can demand. The honest framing: it takes deliberate balancing, and you keep doing it because it's something you'd do anyway. (Several panel answers here were cut by mic dropouts.)

The wrap-up — three simple steps

Betsy closes with the memorable formula. To become an MVP, there are three simple steps: 1. Be an expert. 2. Do what you love (and share it). 3. Let us know — i.e. get nominated (by a Microsoft employee or an existing MVP).

Best advice restated: do what you love, share that, and contribute to the community. The hosts noted they had two more similar sessions that day and the next, and invited the audience to reach out with questions any time during the event.
