# DEM376 — The story of the Global AI Community that spans every continent

> [!info] Session Info
> **Event:** Microsoft Build 2026  
> **Speakers:** Ruland — Cloud Architect by day, Global AI Community board member, Microsoft MVP, based in the Netherlands · Simon — Global AI Community manager / program manager, based in India  
> **Duration:** ~21 min  
> **Watch:** [YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xSEVf01iGvo)
>
> *Note: caption auto-transcription garbles the speaker names. "Ruland" appears as Rulans/Ruland/Rant; the founder "Hank" is almost certainly **Henk Boelman** (a well-known Microsoft AI MVP / Cloud Advocate), captioned "Hank Bulman" — treat that surname as caption-uncertain.*

## 🎯 TL;DR
This is **not a product launch or a demo** — it's the origin story and a how-to-join guide for the **Global AI Community**, a worldwide, grassroots, community-up (not top-down) network of local AI/tech meetups. Started in **2017** by Henk Boelman (caption-uncertain), it has grown to **200,000+ registered members**, **~200 local chapters** across the world, and **4,600+ events run since 2018**, spanning roughly **70 countries**. The model rests entirely on **trusted local chapter leads** who organise city-based events in three flagship formats — **Agent Camps** (hands-on bootcamps, ≤150 people), **Agent Cons** (bigger conferences, 150–1000+ people), and **Global AI Nights** (casual meetups). The core message: there are three easy ways to get involved — **join a local chapter, attend an event, or start your own chapter** — it's open to everyone (not Microsoft-only — AWS/Google folks welcome), the barrier to AI has never been lower, and "you learn by doing, not just by watching."

## 🔑 Key Takeaways
- The Global AI Community is **community-up, not top-down** — it's a brand built by and for its members, run on the side by volunteers, with a dedicated full-time driving force in Simon.
- **It is not about the four board members** — it's about the chapter leads, volunteers, and members. The board is just the supporting HQ.
- The community is **9 years old** (founded 2017), giving it real credibility — new joiners are "stepping onto a train that has already left the station and is growing by the day."
- Scale today: **200,000+ registered members**, **~200 chapters worldwide**, **4,600+ events since 2018**, events run in **~70 countries**.
- **Local chapters are sacred** — every community has different needs, paces, and AI-adoption stages, so local organisers are *fully trusted* and given full leeway, while still getting central support.
- A chapter is **city-based** and should ideally have **at least two chapter leads** (a co-organiser) — running solo burns people out because it's genuine, repeated hard work (venues, speakers, volunteers, sponsors).
- Minimum cadence: **at least one in-person event every 3 months**; the team strongly prefers **monthly** meetups to build rhythm and community. They firmly believe in **in-person** gatherings.
- **Three flagship event formats:** Agent Camps (hands-on bootcamps, ≤150), Agent Cons (conferences, 150–1000+, international speakers), and Global AI Nights (casual "laptop + pizza" meetups).
- **Agent Camps** (formerly "Global AI Bootcamps") are the most-organised yearly event type, now run over a **~3-month window (roughly Feb–April/May)** instead of just March, with a shared **pre-recorded keynote** all chapters watch, plus local workshops. **100+ Agent Camps** were run in a single ~3-month period.
- **Agent Cons** are the big events — in **Mountain View (early May, ~400 people)** there were international speakers from **AWS and Google**; **India** events can exceed **800–1000+ people**.
- **Three ways to get involved:** (1) find/join a local chapter (200 cities), (2) attend any event (in-person/hybrid/virtual) at `globalai.community/events`, (3) start your own chapter if none exists in your city.
- **Starting a chapter** is easy but deliberate: check no chapter already exists (no competing chapters), join the **Discord** and talk to experienced leads, secure a co-organiser, submit a request, get on a call with Global HQ, then receive full onboarding materials.
- **It's explicitly NOT Microsoft-only** — despite being at Microsoft Build, the community is for *everyone*: AWS, Google, any stack. "The Global AI Community is about being a global community for everyone."
- **The barrier to AI has collapsed** — "if you just have a laptop or curiosity, you can build apps" — so the community wants *more* local leaders and hands-on events.
- New chapters/organisers **don't start from scratch**: HQ provides templates, branding, speaker cards, presentations, workshops, and sometimes Azure credits, plus platforms (weekly newsletter, ~50,000-subscriber YouTube channel).
- Cross-chapter **collaboration** is real and encouraged — e.g. a Berlin organiser needing a SharePoint speaker can borrow a connection from a Singapore chapter lead. **"You learn by doing, not just by watching."**

## 📚 Detailed Notes

### Who's presenting and what this session *is not*
Ruland opens by framing his dual life: **"Cloud architect by day, Global AI Community board member by night."** He's based in the **Netherlands** and is a self-described **Microsoft MVP**. He's joined by **Simon**, from **India**, the **Global AI Community manager** (also referred to as program manager) — described warmly as the "dedicated driving factor behind this community."

He's emphatic about the genre: **"This is a different story. This is not a demo. This is not a product launch."** The talk covers what the Global AI Community *is*, how it started, what the team does day-to-day for chapters, how it's organised, and — most importantly — **how the audience can become part of it**, because it's a large, growing community that actively wants more people.

### The board — and why it's "not about us"
Simon introduces the board:
- **Hank / Henk** (founder — surname captioned "Bulman", almost certainly **Henk Boelman**) — the person who actually **started** the Global AI Community.
- **Sammy** — board member.
- **Ruland** — board member (and the one telling the origin story).
- **Simon** — himself, working as program manager for the community.

But the repeated, deliberate message: **"this community is more about just these four people… it's about the chapter leads, community members, [and] volunteers."** The board is HQ/support scaffolding; the community is the people in the cities.

### Origin story — 2017 to today
- **2017 — the beginning.** "AI was cool, but there was no Gen AI" yet. People learned differently; meetups existed but were **uneven and city-dependent** — some cities ran excellent meetups with great speakers; others were just figuring it out, often student-heavy (which they frame positively — "the more students the better") but with few events. It was simply "a different time to be in."
- **2018 — first big wave.** The first major wave of **bootcamps**, which became a recurring flagship.
- **Then it just grew and grew** — including **through COVID**, where they "still remained growing."
- **The defining principle:** *"It's not like a top-down brand. We are community up."* They genuinely care about the community, and everyone runs it on the side — except Simon, the dedicated driver.

### The numbers (the "big numbers" Simon teased)
From "a very humble beginning," today (per the **globalai.community** website):
- **200,000+ members** registered on the platform.
- **~200 chapters** around the world.
- **4,600+ events** run since 2018.
- Events run in roughly **70 countries**.
- The "best part": these are **locals** — local chapter leads organising events **in their own cities**, coming together to share experiences and learn.

Anyone can go to **`globalai.community`** (spoken as "globala.com community"), find their city, and join that chapter.

### How it's organised — the gospel of local chapters
This is flagged as **"one of the most important messages we try to get across."** They **firmly believe in local chapters** because:
- Every community worldwide has **different needs**, runs at a **different pace**, and is at a **different stage of adopting AI**.
- Local communities know best whether they need more **training**, **hands-on workshops**, or **bootcamps**.

Therefore local organisers are **fully trusted** and given **all the leeway they want** — *"We fully trust them"* — while still receiving central support. Ruland's point: **you can't run a community at scale without supporting the local chapters**, and they all carry the **same message/story** even through "all those voices." There are many things HQ does to support them (covered later).

### What a "chapter" actually is
- A chapter is **local / city-based**, enabling a local community to get together, share experiences, and learn.
- **Strong encouragement to have at least two chapter leads** (a co-organiser), because running a chapter is genuinely hard: you have to **find a venue, look for speakers, recruit volunteers, and sometimes find sponsors.**
- To keep a chapter active: organise **at least one in-person event every 3 months.**
- Event formats are flexible — **virtual, hybrid, or in-person** — but **in-person is highly encouraged.**
- **It's open to anybody.** Because AI has lowered the barrier to development, *"if you just have a laptop or curiosity, you can build apps."*
- Ruland adds: one event per 3 months is the **bare minimum**; they prefer a **monthly meetup** to build rhythm and local community. **Shout-out to the Berlin chapter** — one of the older and best-performing chapters, "really doing a good job with their events."

### The three event formats
There are a few moments each year when all chapters come together — **not physically**, but running the *same* event simultaneously across their cities:

**1. Agent Camps** (the flagship; formerly **Global AI Bootcamps**)
- Not huge — **usually up to ~150 people**, but can take many forms/sizes.
- **All about hands-on learning** — workshops to get started building with AI and agents, plus sessions.
- Run **worldwide across ~70 countries.**
- Run over a **multi-week-to-multi-month period** (see deep-dive below).
- The **most-organised event type** in the community.

**2. Agent Cons** (the bigger events)
- More like **conferences**, **usually 150+ people**, ranging from indie up to very large.
- Some Agent Cons reach **~1,000 people**, and **India even more**.
- Draw **international speakers**, focused on learning about AI.

**3. Global AI Nights** (the general meetup format)
- The most common, casual format — a **"meetup feel."**
- Takes many forms/sizes, ideal for **getting started**: *"everything goes — if you're there, you have a laptop, maybe you bring something to eat or order some pizza, and you just have a good time learning from each other."*
- Nothing is enforced, but chapters are **highly encouraged to organise at least the Agent Camp.**

### Agent Camp vs Agent Con — the difference (answered in Q&A)
This distinction was asked about directly, so Ruland clarified at length:
- **Agent Camp = the bootcamp.** Hands-on, designed to **get people started**. Usually smaller — **typically <150 people** (can be bigger). It's their **yearly event**, formerly run only in **March**, now stretched to a **~3-month window (roughly February to April/May)** because one month was too little — global events, holidays, and regional concerns had to be accommodated. Includes lots of **workshops**, some sessions, and a **pre-recorded keynote** that everyone watches (keeps "the thread of the message" consistent), while local communities still own the organising.
- **Agent Con = the bigger event.** They **fly in international speakers**, with one or two workshops but **mainly knowledge-sharing** on AI topics. Example: an **Agent Con in Mountain View** (~**May 4**, "last month"), about **400 people**, with **international speakers from AWS and Google**. India events can exceed **800** (last attendance cited as "up to 800"); typically they run **between 150 and 300** people. Both are ultimately "about AI and agents" — similar themes, very different scale.

### How people actually join — the community flywheel
The structure is described as "purely logical": people who want to get started **join one of the channels** (notably **Discord**), **attend an event**, and **talk to the chapter leads and people there.** From there they **find their chapter, or start their own.** It's the community **helping each other to grow the community further.**

HQ offers substantial support to make this work:
- **Getting-started resources**, **templating**, **branding**, and **platforms**.
- A **weekly newsletter.**
- A **YouTube channel** with **~50,000 subscribers** ("like and subscribe").
- Platforms for chapters to **voice their sessions and community.**
- **Credibility** — *"we've existed now for nine years"* — a community of real size; new joiners are boarding a moving, growing train.

### Three ways to get involved
1. **Find a local chapter** — there are chapters in **200 cities** worldwide; if there's one in your city, join it.
2. **Attend an event** — you don't need to be a chapter lead. Go to **`globalai.community/events`**, find one of the many events worldwide (in-person, hybrid, or virtual), attend, build connections, then maybe join a nearby chapter.
3. **Start a chapter** — if none exists in your city, *you* can be the chapter lead: fill out a **form**, get on a **call with the Global HQ team** (they want to know who you are and your expectations), then receive **all the content** and a **nice onboarding email/process** to start building your regional community.

Even if a chapter already exists, **reach out to the current leads** and offer to help/join — grow the existing chapter rather than competing.

### Steps to start a chapter (the detailed playbook)
1. **Check if a chapter already exists** in your region — **no competing chapters**; if one exists, reach out to the local organisers there instead.
2. **Join the Discord first** — reach out to existing chapter organisers: *how did you get started? what should I do next? what should I prepare?* Then go through the **onboarding process.**
3. **Get a co-organiser — critical.** *"You cannot do this alone."* They've **seen chapters fail**: people start enthusiastically, but it's hard work done **frequently/monthly** (sponsors, locations, speakers). The first one or two events are easy; by your third/fourth event — or three years in — life happens, so you need a **second organiser (or more).** Chapters with more than two organisers are "totally fine."
4. **Submit your request** — then **be patient.** HQ has to **approve** it, understand your motivation and interest, and figure out how to help. *"It's really about supporting you."*
5. **Once approved**, HQ **sends all the materials** and you're good to go — start with something **hybrid or virtual** first if you like, though **in-person is strongly preferred** ("we really believe in building communities in person").

### What's next — the call to action
- AI is **moving fast** and the **barrier to entry has come down**, so the community **needs more local chapters and more leaders** willing to start a community and run **hands-on events** on the latest updates.
- Concrete example: if you ran a chapter, you'd take everything learned **at Microsoft Build** back to your city and **share it with your community** — and **collaborate with other chapter leads.**
- **Cross-chapter collaboration is real:** running a virtual event in **Berlin** and need a **SharePoint** speaker? Reach out to a **Singapore** chapter lead who ran a similar event months ago for the speaker connection. Many chapters work together this way.
- Mantra: **"You learn by doing, not just by watching."** It's not easy — you give up weekends — but **bring others**: if a colleague is interested in AI, bring them to a meetup; if you know someone who'd make a good organiser, encourage them and point them to Global AI as the platform to start.
- **It's very rewarding** — *"the community always gives back."* There are many stories of the community coming together; ask the chapter leads.
- A bigger purpose: **help people make sense of AI.** Things move so fast that many feel **overwhelmed or even scared**; a community can help people **find their way in AI.**
- **The most important slide:** *"It's about you."* They're explicitly **not** there to pressure anyone to join — they simply, firmly believe the community can **add something to your AI experience.**

### Not just Microsoft — and housekeeping
- Ruland (a self-professed Microsoft MVP who loves Microsoft tech) stresses the irony of saying this at Build: **you don't have to be heavily invested in Microsoft technology.** If you're into **Google** or **AWS**, you can join too — *"The Global AI Community is about being a global community for everyone."*
- Practical nudge: you're at Build, so maybe take it easy this month, but **find an event next month** — there's always something going on — **or start your own chapter.** And **bring someone** (a colleague interested in AI).
- **At Build specifically:** there's a **table talk tomorrow at 9:30 a.m.**, plus the team is around for **two days** at the **expert booth** — come ask questions any time.
- The session closes with a Q&A (the Agent Camp vs Agent Con question above) and thanks.
